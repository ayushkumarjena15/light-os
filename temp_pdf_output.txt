1. The Problem : 
1.1 Problem Overview:  
Our targeted problem is the traditional public lighting system  that operates  across 
the country . In 2015 the government of India  launched an initiative for the public 
light infrastructure,  Street Lighting National Program (SLNP) . The pro gram aimed to 
replac e Conventional Street  light  with Energy Efficient  and Smart LED light  all 
across the nation . In addition, solar street lights are also being deployed in poor -
connectivity regions , even  though after all these initiatives , the ground -level reality 
still faces major inefficienc ies in operation, monitoring and maintenances  
 
1.2  Key P roblem s and Issues:  
1. Manual Intervention  and Irregular Inspection : 
• Streetlights are still operated manually which requires human resources 
therefore increasing the operational costs.  
• Due t o lack of discipline  or accountab ility, street lights  often  
function  at wrong time , delay ed at night  operation  time or may left 
active during the day.  
• Field Observation  in rural areas shows that over 70% of streetlights function 
irregular ly, which means either running during day time or remaining OFF at 
night.  
• Handling thousands of streetlights,  with timely activation and deactivation 
seems challenging without proper budget allocation and manpower , causes 
poor maintena nces , month  long  outages and raises security and safety 
concerns.  
• Due to irregular or 24-hour  operation  of lights,  the life expectancy decreases 
by up to  60% , increasing  both power consumption and frequent 
replacements  costs . 
 
1.3  Existing  Infrastructure and Challenges  
1. Control Switch es:                                                                                          
• Conventional control switches  require  an extra parallel  cabling , which is not 
feasible  under modern single line distribution plans of different , state electricity 
distribution       companies.  
2. LDR-Based Systems:  
• Light Dependent  Resistors (LDRs) are not that reliable  when it comes to  tracking 
the light failures , and differentiating between natural day light and  artificial light  
conditions.   
3.  Smart City-Focused I nitiative :  
• Existing smart streetlight solution s are particularly focusing on smart cities, as 
integration or replacement of traditional infrastructure complex and economically 
unviable for rural and semi -rural areas .

3. The Solution:  
 
The LightOS brings  a economical and optimized  approach  for Indian  public  light  
infrastructur al and development by incorporating  advanced  technologies  such  as IoT, 
LoRa,  Wi-Fi Mesh,  Satellite Communication and Artificial Intelligence and Machine  
learning . 
 
                    3.1 Technological  Overview  
 
a. Real -Time Automation:  
• Unlike traditional systems that rely on manual switching, LDR            circuits or basic  
timers,  LightOS  includes  real-time  automation,  utilizing  sensors  and 
programmable logic to adjust lighting based on environmental conditions.  
• This ensures lights are active only when needed, drastically reducing 
unnecessary  electricity consumption. The system  can also adapt to  local 
variations in  sunrise and  sunset times, offering a more dynamic and 
efficient solution than Traditional  lighting  systems.  
b.  Fault  Detection  and Reporting:   
• One of the impressive  features  of SPLS  is its built -in fault  detection  mechanism.  The 
system  continuously  monitors  the status  of each streetlight and automatically 
identifies malfunctions such as burnt -out bulbs or  wiring  issues.   
• This is communicated  to a central  system,  reducing  time  of light  and late 
maintenance issues. Traditional street lighting often requires manual  inspections, 
leading to delays in repairs and extended periods of darkness in public  areas.  
c.  Energy  Efficiency  through  Advanced  Control:   
• LightOS  leverages energy -efficient  technologies,  including  the use of LED lighting  
combined  with  automated  control systems.  
• By switching  lights  on and off based  on real-time  data,  energy  savings  are 
maximized,  directly  reducing  electricity  bills.  
d.  Scalability  and Flexibility:   
• The system  is designed  to be easily  scalable,  capable  of being  deployed  across  
both  rural  and urban  settings  with  minimal  infrastructure changes.   
• Its flexible  architecture  allows  for the integration  of various  communication  
protocols, whether in areas with access to stable internet or in rural zones using  
mesh  networks  or local  Wi-Fi solutions . 
e. Integration  with  Existing  technology:   
• LightOS is easily  compatible  with  the existing  streetlight  and can be easily  installed  
across  the operating  streetlight.  An enclosed  device  will be provided  that will be best  
suited  for the running  streetlight . 
f. Sustainability  and Environmental  Impact:   
• By reducing  energy  waste  and optimizing  streetlight  operation,  LightOS  supports  
broader  sustainability  goals,  contributing  to the reduction of greenhouse gas 
emissions. Municipalities and rural Gram panchayats  can benefit  from  significant  
cost savings  and also promote  eco-friendly  practices .

 4. The market  potential  
 
The Indian Smart Street Lighting Industry  is undergoing a rapid digital transformation 
driven by the push for  energy efficiency, automation, and sustainable infrastructure.   
According to industry estimates, the Indian smart street lighting market was valued at  USD 
58.3 million in 2024  and is projected to grow to  USD 455 million by 2035, registering a  CAGR 
of ~20.3% (2025 –2035).  
This market encompasses the  hardware, software, connectivity, and integrated service 
ecosystem  required for intelligent street lighting control — including  remote monitoring, 
automated scheduling, real -time fault detection,  and energy optimization.   
 
 4.1 Target Customer Segments   
SPLS (Smart Public Lighting System) is designed to serve multiple high -potential verticals:   
•  Urban Local Bodies (ULBs)  and Municipal Corporations  under the  Smart Cities Mission   
• Government -led programs  such as the  Street Lighting National Programme (SLNP)   
• Private infrastructure developers,  industrial parks,  special economic zones (SEZs), and  gated 
communities   
• Rural and hilly regions, where  standalone or low -connectivity  lighting automation is critical 
for safety and cost efficiency       
  
4.2 Market Potential Analysis   
India currently operates over  45+ million public street lights, with nearly  60% (≈ 27 
million)  identified as potential candidates for smart automation over the next decade.

10. Future Vision  
As the system evolves towards Version 4, and then Version 5, LightOS will certainly 
integrate artificial intelligence. Real -time data analytics capabilities will be included too. 
This  integration enables certain advantages:  
10.1 AI  Readiness  
• Predictive Energy Management:  Continuous monitoring of consumption helps predict 
usage patterns. It allows automatic optimization of brightness levels. These brightness 
adjustments are based on time, traffic and also the current weather conditions.  
• Proactive Maintenance:  AI models will analyze fault data. They do so to forecast 
potential failures. Downtime will be reduced, not to mention maintenance costs, but

10.2 Open Architecture & IoT Integration  
LightOS is built, as it stands, on an open architecture; and a modular one. Allowing it 
seamless integration, we believe, with other smart infrastructure systems in time. Soon 
enough, the same LightOS -enabled poles should be able to act as multi -purpose IoT 
gateways, even supporting possibilities like:  
• Public Safety Integration:  Cameras and various sensors can now monitor pedestrian 
movement and traffic conditions; also important is monitoring public security in 
real-time which means quickly addressing risks to our community.    
  
 
 
          • Environmental Monitoring:  Sensors will also integrate air quality and temperature 
metrics; and noise levels too in an attempt to provide, hopefully actionable, data on 
our collective impact. This is useful data, of course, for future city planning.  
• Smart City Expansion:  These IoT -equipped poles can be said to form the backbone. 
Certainly, they will for a broader smart infrastructure: they enable applications, say 
like, waste management. Furthermore, they help to establish smart parking areas 
and support of EV charging stations.  
10.3 Business Perspective  
The SPLS business model it is designed for both sustainability and for scalability as well. Future 
initiatives surely include, eventually:  
• On-Site Maintenance Teams:  Dedicated service units shall, in all likelihood, be 
deployed regionally so that the response becomes rapid. Not just responding to issues; 
a focus on preventing system failure, maintaining uptime and even system reliability 
will be included.  
• Data as a Service (DaaS):  The system will need scale, when scaling up; LightOS could 
collect precious data. Think, on energy usage; even data collected on lighting 
performance and of course urban activity! This data once made anonymous and 
aggregated, could actually then be sold; to city hall and agencies needing solid metrics 
upon which solid smart policy might exist in reality.  
10.4  Sustainability & Environmental Contribution  
LightOS  can definitely support India’s push, to its sustainability goals, it can assist their 
commitment to becoming a Green Country. All goals can be met.  
• Reduction in CO₂ Emissions:  Well -thought -out lighting schedules coupled; with well 
executed, effective dimming can potentially cut a good amount; off, in particular 
about 30 -40% energy reduction, lowering our carbon imprint.  
• Cost effectiveness:  affordability to most places even really tiny towns or smaller 
places is our sincere hope; they need it the most really.
